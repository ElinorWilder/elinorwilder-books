Character Notes

Connor: Rejection Sensitivity Dysphoria and Sensory Processing Disorder

Connor experiences rejection sensitivity dysphoria (RSD), a condition where rejection feels catastrophically painful—far beyond typical disappointment. It's often unrecognized in men. Connor's pattern of expecting abandonment and misreading neutral situations as rejection reflects authentic lived experience. He also has sensory processing differences that affect how he experiences touch and emotional overwhelm (Sensory Processing Disorder).

Emma: Autism

Emma represents autistic characteristics including direct communication, sensory sensitivity, and different emotional processing patterns. Her scientific approach to relationships and delayed recognition of romantic feelings reflect authentic autistic experiences often overlooked in popular media.

Annelie: Twice-Exceptional (2e) - Gifted with Dyslexia

Annelie represents what psychologists call "twice-exceptional" or 2e—individuals who are both intellectually gifted and have a learning disability. Her mathematical genius exists alongside severe dyslexia, creating a complex cognitive profile that's often misunderstood.

What dyslexia is: A language-based learning disability affecting reading, writing, and spelling. It has nothing to do with intelligence, emotional processing, or social skills. Annelie's struggles with written language are neurological, not a reflection of her intellectual capacity.

What giftedness brings: Pattern recognition abilities that operate at genius level. Annelie sees mathematical relationships, spatial patterns, and logical systems that others miss entirely. Her brain processes abstract concepts with extraordinary speed and precision.

The 2e experience: Being twice-exceptional creates unique challenges. In childhood, she was perceived as smart until she couldn't read grade-level texts or began failing classes. Then she was incorrectly labeled as lazy or unmotivated. As she grew up and moved into a working environment, where asking for accommodations was not acceptable, she lives with constant vigilance of being 'exposed'. Annelie understands Reed's trauma so well because she has experienced hypervigilance, threat, anxiety and the exhaustion that comes with constantly tracking your surroundings.

High intelligence and social processing: Annelie processes at a fundamentally different speed than most people around her. Her ability to solve structural engineering problems through mental math in seconds, her exceptional working memory, her processing speed—these don't exist in isolation. They mean the rest of the world feels slow to her. She sees patterns, complexities, and implications instantly that others take minutes or hours to grasp. This creates profound isolation even when she reads social situations accurately. Her brilliance sets her apart, making genuine connection difficult despite her strong people-reading abilities.

Annelie's journey reflects authentic twice-exceptional experience: brilliant, capable, and constantly navigating a world that revolves around the written medium.

Reed: Complex Trauma, Language Impairment, and Misunderstood Strength

Reed represents one of the most clinically complex presentations I could write. A man whose difficulties are invisible because he appears "gruff" and self-contained by nature. While he is inherently a stoic man, he's also navigating profound neurological and trauma-based challenges.

Complex PTSD (C-PTSD): Unlike single-incident PTSD, C-PTSD develops from prolonged, repeated trauma. Reed experienced childhood trauma within his shifter pack, ongoing trauma related to his shifter identity and exile, and then military combat trauma. C-PTSD affects emotional regulation, self-perception, relationships, and the ability to feel safe in the world. His hypervigilance, emotional numbing, and catastrophic self-blame reflect authentic C-PTSD patterns.

Language and Communication Impairment: Reed's brain processes language differently. He's extremely literal, misses subtext and implications, struggles with verbal expression, and relies on scripted responses in social situations.

His language impairment can feel like "pulling teeth" to communication partners. Conversations require more effort, explanations need to be explicit, and social misunderstandings are frequent. This creates relationship difficulties not because Reed doesn't care, but because his brain doesn't process verbal and social communication the way neurotypical brains do.

Diagnostic complexity: Reed shows many characteristics associated with autism spectrum: literal language processing, difficulty with social scripts, sensory sensitivities, need for structure and routine, but doesn't meet full diagnostic criteria. He exists in that challenging clinical space where he has significant difficulties but doesn't fit neat diagnostic categories. This makes him harder to understand and support.

Strength as isolation: Reed's exceptional physical strength and rigid moral code create an additional layer of isolation. Society perceives large, strong men as potential threats. His strength makes others uncomfortable, his moral boundaries make him appear inflexible, and his inability to explain himself verbally means he can't easily correct misperceptions. This creates profound loneliness and reinforces his belief that he doesn't belong.

Reed's journey reflects the experiences of brilliant, capable, and stoic men who are constantly navigating a world that works through the spoken word. I especially wanted to write a romance hero who cannot flirt and smooth talk or make sweeping, grand love confessions. I paired a man who cannot process spoken language well with a woman who cannot process written language.

A Note on Representation

These characters represent individual experiences, not entire communities. Every person with autism, SPD, dyslexia, giftedness, C-PTSD, or language impairment has a unique profile. Reed and Annelie's specific challenges and strengths are theirs alone.

As a neurodivergent psychologist, I write from both lived and professional experience. I hope you enjoyed reading their story and have a deeper understanding of how neurological differences, trauma, and misperception shape lives, and how the right partner can see past all of that to the person underneath.

Character notes for other couples in the series will be added after their books are published. I believe readers should meet the characters first, experience their journeys authentically, and form their own connections before learning the clinical frameworks behind their portrayals.
